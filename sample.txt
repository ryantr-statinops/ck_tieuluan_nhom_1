    Many numerical methods have been developed for the PNP and NSPNP equations. It is crucial to preserve some important physical
properties at the discrete level, such as the mass conservation, concentration positivity and energy stability, and the related work
can be referred to [12–27]. In addition, some numerical techniques and implementation strategies were proposed to improve the
performance eﬃciency, such as the adaptive ﬁnite element methods [28–30], the two grid methods [31], the stabilized techniques
[32,33], the proper orthogonal decomposition method [34], and some improved iteration techniques [35–38].
    There are some studies about the error estimates of the ﬁnite element method for the PNP and NSPNP equations. Sun and He
analyzed an implicit Crank-Nicolson ﬁnite element scheme for the PNP equations in [39], and obtained the optimal error estimates
in 𝑙∞ (𝐻 1 ) norm for all variables, however, the convergence order of 𝑙∞ (𝐿2 ) norm was suboptimal. They also proposed the mixed
ﬁnite element method for the PNP equations [40], and obtained optimal error estimates for 𝑙∞ (𝐻 1 ) and 𝑙∞ (𝐿2 ) norms, and later
they discussed the PNP-Stokes equations and modiﬁed NSPNP equations [41,42]. Shi and Yang et al. investigated the conforming
and nonconforming ﬁnite element methods on rectangular meshes [43,44], and obtained the superconvergence results by integral
identities techniques. Yang et al. researched the virtual element method on general polygonal meshes [45], and derived the error
estimates in 𝑙∞ (𝐻 1 ) and 𝑙∞ (𝐿2 ) norms. However, due to the use of inverse inequality and mathematical inductive argument, a
mesh restriction is needed in the above references [39–45]. Gao and He [46,47] presented linearized conservative ﬁnite element
and mixed ﬁnite element methods for the PNP equations, and established unconditionally optimal error estimates for all variables
in 𝑙2 (𝐻 1 ) and 𝑙∞ (𝐿2 ) norms, but they do not derive the error estimates in 𝑙∞ (𝐻 1 ) norm. For the convergence analysis and error
estimates of some other gradient ﬂow model coupled with ﬂuid motion, such as the Cahn-Hilliard-Hele-Shaw equations and the
Cahn-Hilliard-Navier-Stokes system, one can refer to [48–51].
    In this paper, we focus on the error estimates of the linearized backward Euler ﬁnite element scheme for the NSPNP equations,
and use low order conforming ﬁnite element pairs. Firstly, under an inductive hypothesis, we derive the existence and uniqueness
of solutions, and the unconditional error estimates of all variables in 𝑙∞ (𝐿2 ) norms. Then, we obtain the 𝑙∞ (𝐻 1 ) and 𝑙2 (𝐻 2 ) norm
stability of the ﬁnite element solutions. Next, based on the stability, we deduce the unconditional error estimates in 𝑙∞ (𝐻 1 ) norm.
Moreover, the global mass conservation and the energy stability are also derived for the proposed numerical scheme. Finally, several
numerical examples are implemented to verify the theoretical analysis. Compared with the results in [39–45], we remove the mesh
restriction.
    Throughout the article, the notations of the standard Sobolev spaces are used. The generic positive constant 𝐶 with or without
subscript is independent of the mesh size ℎ and the time step 𝜏 .

7. Conclusion and future work

   In this work, we present a linearized backward Euler ﬁnite element scheme for the NSPNP equations, and prove the existence
and uniqueness of this scheme. Then, we obtain the unconditional error estimates of all variables in 𝑙∞ (𝐿2 ) and 𝑙∞ (𝐻 1 ) norms.
Moreover, we give two physical properties: the global mass conservation and energy stability. However, the concentration positivity
cannot be theoretically justiﬁed, which is also important for the numerical scheme of the NSPNP equations. In fact, for the pure PNP
equations, there has been some existing numerical methods which keeps these physical properties. In the future, we will try to apply
these methods to the NSPNP equations. In addition, our numerical scheme is only ﬁrst order accurate in time, and in a forthcoming
research we plan to propose some second order accurate numerical schemes for the NSPNP equations, such as Crank-Nicolson scheme
and BDF2 scheme. Finally, it is interesting and challenging to construct robust schemes to solve some practice problems, such as
charging dynamics in a nanopore, electroosmotic ﬂow in a nanochannel, electroconvective ﬂow on an ion-selective surface [56,60],
and so on.
